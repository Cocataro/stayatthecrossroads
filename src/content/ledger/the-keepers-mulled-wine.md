She doesn't measure.

This is the thing that frustrates the guests who want the recipe written out, the ones who cup their hands around a warm mug and say, *please, just tell me how.* The keeper watches them the way she watches everyone — with a patience that could outlast a weather system — and then she says the same thing she always says.

"You'll know when it smells right."

But she wrote it down once, for a young woman who was heading north into the cold and would have no inn waiting at the other end. The keeper folded the paper and tucked it into the woman's coat pocket and said, *Don't lose that.*

The woman didn't.

And here it is:

---

**The Inn's Mulled Wine**  
*Serves 6. Good for cold nights and cold news.*

- 1 bottle red wine (full-bodied; the keeper favors the darker sort)
- 2 cups water
- 2 cinnamon sticks
- 6 whole cloves
- 2 allspice berries
- 1 strip of orange peel, pith removed
- 3 tablespoons honey, or more if the wine is sharp
- A blade of mace, if you have one

Combine everything in a pot. Warm over low heat — do not boil; the keeper is particular about this — until steam rises and the kitchen smells like a place worth returning to. Hold at that temperature for twenty minutes. Strain. Serve in mugs that have weight to them.

The keeper adds something else, always. No one has managed to identify what it is. The recipe above will get you nine-tenths of the way there. The last tenth, she says, is not for writing down.

---

*Adapted from Hannah Glasse, "The Art of Cookery Made Plain and Easy" (1747). Glasse's original receipt calls for claret warmed with spices and a hot poker; the Crossroads version uses a slow heat and swaps nutmeg for allspice.*
