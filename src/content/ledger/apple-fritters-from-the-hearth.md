The keeper makes them in autumn.

The apples come in on the valley road — brought by the same family for three generations, father to son to daughter, a cart with a loose wheel that always sounds like it's about to give — and the keeper buys a full crate and sets it in the cellar. The fritters appear in October, without announcement, whenever the fire is going and there's a lull in the dinner hour.

The smell is the announcement. It brings people in from the courtyard.

She has never been asked for the recipe. No one thought to ask until a girl from the village, who was learning to cook for her grandmother's household, finally said: *Will you show me?*

The keeper showed her.

---

**Apple Fritters**  
*Serves 4 to 6, depending on the apples and the appetite.*

- 3 or 4 firm apples (the keeper prefers the tart ones)
- 1 cup flour
- 1½ teaspoons baking powder
- ¼ teaspoon salt
- 1 egg, beaten
- ½ cup milk
- Oil or lard for frying
- Powdered sugar or warm honey, for finishing

Peel, core, and cut the apples into rounds about half an inch thick. Pat them dry — this matters; the keeper is firm on it.

In a bowl, whisk together the flour, baking powder, and salt. Add the beaten egg and milk and stir until a smooth batter forms. Dip each apple round in the batter, letting the excess fall away.

Fry in oil over medium-high heat — enough oil that the fritters float — turning once, until golden on both sides. Two to three minutes per side. Do not crowd the pan.

Drain on cloth. Dust with powdered sugar or drizzle with warm honey. Serve immediately. They do not keep.

The keeper serves them with a look that says *eat them now.* You should eat them now.

---

*Adapted from Mrs. Isabella Beeton, "The Book of Household Management" (1861), Recipe No. 1375. Beeton's original calls for frying in hot lard and serving with sifted sugar; the Crossroads version updates the proportions and adds baking powder.*
