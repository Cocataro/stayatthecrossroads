## Email 2 — Day 2

**Subject:** The part I was afraid to write
**Preview text:** Every book has one scene the writer almost deleted.

---

I hope the first three chapters found you well. (And that the soup scene in Chapter 2 made you hungry. It makes *me* hungry, and I wrote it.)

I wanted to tell you about the scene I almost cut.

There's a moment in the book where Maren is alone in the kitchen after the last guest has gone to bed. She's washing dishes. That's it. No magic. No plot. Just a woman washing dishes in a quiet inn, listening to the building settle.

My first instinct was to cut it. "Nothing happens," I thought. "Move on to the next scene."

But I left it in. Because that's the whole point of the Crossroads Inn — the quiet moments matter. The dishes matter. The sound of a building settling around you matters.

If you picked up this book hoping for a quiet place to rest, that scene is for you.

## Email 3 — Day 5

**Subject:** Books that feel like this one
**Preview text:** If you liked the inn, you'll love these.

---

People ask me what I was reading when I started *The Crossroads Inn.* Fair question. Here's the honest answer:

**Legends & Lattes** by Travis Baldree — the book that proved "cozy fantasy" was a thing people wanted. A barbarian opens a coffee shop. I read it in one sitting and thought: *what if the shop was an inn, and the barbarian was tired of fighting?*

**Can't Spell Treason Without Tea** by Rebecca Thorne — two women leave the royal court and open a bookshop. The romance is gentle. The magic is soft. The tea is constant.

**The House in the Cerulean Sea** by TJ Klune — not fantasy in the sword-and-sorcery sense, but it has the same warmth. Found family. A building that feels alive. The conviction that kindness is not weakness.

**A Psalm for the Wild-Built** by Becky Chambers — a tea monk and a robot sit down and talk about what matters. That's the whole book. It's perfect.

If you haven't read any of these, start with whichever title made you feel something just now. They're all the kind of book you can read in a single rainy afternoon.

— Jason

## Email 4 — Day 8

**Subject:** Why the inn has five roads
**Preview text:** The world-building detail that changed the whole series.

---

Quick one today.

When I first sketched the inn, it sat at a crossroads of two roads. Standard fantasy geography. North-south, east-west. Simple.

But something was off. Two roads means two kinds of traveler — coming and going. That's not enough. An inn needs *variety.* It needs the merchant who's lost, the soldier who's running, the scholar who's searching, the pilgrim who's arrived, and the wanderer who doesn't know which one they are yet.

Five roads. Five kinds of stranger. Five reasons to open the door.

The moment I changed the map, the book changed with it. Suddenly the inn wasn't just a building — it was a *junction.* A place where stories crossed. Where the bard from the northern road could meet the merchant from the eastern pass and neither of them would be the same after.

That's when I knew I had a series, not just a book.

Six books. Five roads. One inn.

More soon.

— Jason
